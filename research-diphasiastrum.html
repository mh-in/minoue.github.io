Research · Topic

Club mosses (Diphasiastrum, Lycopodiaceae)

The genus Diphasiastrum belongs to one of the oldest lineages of extant terrestrial plants, the class Lycopodiophyta (also known as Lycophyta)1. In systematics, Diphasiastrum has been recognised as a section of the genus Lycopodium L. (Lycopodium sect. Complanata), but due to certain features — branchlet structure, chromosome number (n = 23), and carrot-shaped gametophytes — it is now treated as a genus of its own2. With 27 described taxa3, including hybrids, Diphasiastrum is the most complex group within the Lycopodiaceae and is distributed widely throughout the Northern Hemisphere.

Unlike many other plants that commonly undergo allopolyploid hybridization resulting in numerous polyploid individuals, Diphasiastrum exhibits only few polyploid individuals4,5,6 and homoploid hybrid speciation (diploid homoploid species) is supposedly predominant — even though some hybrid taxa seem to be originating from polyploid hybridization (e.g. D. × wightianum, D. × zanclophyllum, and D. × yueshanense)4,7.

In homoploid hybridization, backcrossing occurs more easily than in allopolyploid hybridization, resulting in frequent introgression6,8 and, therefore, contributing to species diversification. This relatively unique evolutionary mechanism among plants provides a valuable model for studying homoploid hybrid speciation and its role in adaptive radiation.

My research addresses the phylogeographic and genomic dimensions of this reticulate system across the Holarctic. Using organellar and nuclear markers at different evolutionary resolutions, I investigate how post-glacial range dynamics, cytonuclear discordance, and the distinctive reproductive biology of lycophytes — where both chloroplast and nuclear genomes pass through a free-living gametophyte stage — shape genetic structure and hybrid directionality. A central aim is to connect species-level phylogeographic patterns with the mechanisms that govern hybridization outcomes.

Current projects

  1. Population genetics and biogeography of Diphasiastrum complanatum in the Northern Hemisphere. Using chloroplast microsatellites and the nuclear marker RPB2 across >500 samples from Eurasia and North America, this study examines spatial genetic structure and cytonuclear discordance to reconstruct post-glacial history and current population connectivity.
  2. Reticulate evolution and cryptic diversity of Diphasiastrum in East Asia. Building on phylogenetic findings from Schnittler et al. (2024), this project investigates species boundaries, hybrid directionality, and parental contributions in East Asian Diphasiastrum, where multiple species co-occur and form complex hybrid assemblages — including some of the few known polyploid taxa in the genus — that remain largely unresolved. The work integrates molecular markers, flow cytometry, and genome-wide approaches to clarify patterns of reticulate evolution in this region.
  3. Status of Diphasiastrum × issleri in Central Europe. Using genome-wide SNP data (MIG-seq), this collaborative study investigates the genomic composition, parental contributions, and directionality of hybridisation in this widespread homoploid hybrid between D. alpinum and D. complanatum.
